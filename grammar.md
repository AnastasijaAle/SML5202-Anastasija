<h1> German Definite Articles </h1>

<p> German defnite articles are essentially one of the most important grammatical parts as these are the equivalent of 'the' in the English language. <br>
  As seen in the table below, there are diferrent articles depending on which case the sentence you write is and which gender the word is. <br> </p>
  
  Definite articles are used before nouns, for direct objects , certain people (like family members) and ideas. <br>
  
<table>
  <tr><th> </th><th> Masculine </th> <th> Feminine </th> <th> Neuter </th><th> Plural</th> </tr>
  <tr><td> Nominative </td><td> der </td> <td> die</td> <td> das </td><td> die</td></tr>
  <tr><td> Accusative</td><td> den </td><td> die </td><td> das </td><td> die </td> </tr>
  <tr><td> Genitive </td><td> des </td><td> der </td><td> des </td><td> der </td> </tr>
  <tr><td>Dative </td><td> dem </td><td> der</td><td> des </td><td> der </td> </tr>
  </table>
  
  At the moment, it is only important to learn the Nominative ones which are :
<ul>  <Li> DER - Masculine </li>
<Li>  DIE - Feminine  </li>
<Li>  DAS - Neuter </li> 
  <Li> DIE - Plural </li> </ul>
  
  Eventually though, the whole table below will need to be memorised as these are used so often in everyday speech and writing that learning it will just be much more convenient.

<h1> German Indefinite Articles </h1>

<p> Similarily to definite articles, indefinite articles are used for nouns, objects and certail people but this time, they are used for <b> INDIRECT </B> nouns, obejcst and certain people. <br>
  
German indefinite articles are equivalent to the English 'a' and 'an'.
  
<br>

Luckily, this time, there are no articles for plural and therefore there is less to learn.
<br> </p>

<table><tr><th> </th><th> Masculine </th> <th> Feminine </th> <th> Neuter </th><th> Plural</th> </tr>

  <tr><td> Accusative</td><td>einen</td><td>eine</td><td>ein</td><td> -</td> </tr>
  <tr><td> Genitive </td><td>eines</td><td>einer</td><td>eines</td><td> -</td> </tr>

  </table>

  <h1> Grammar Cases </h1>
  
 <p> Just like any other language, German has cases. </p>
  
  
  German has 4(four) cases that include:
 <Ul> <li> Nominative </li>
<Li> Accusative  </li>
<Li> Dative  </li> 
  <Li>  Genitive </li> </ul>
  
  <p> Each case has a purpose and below I will go into more detail on each of them. It is important to note that here, I will cover the basic definitions of each case and will not go into too much detail as there are a lot of other exceptions and rules for cases. <br>

I therefore reccomend going to the pages 'Where Next' and 'Resources' to continue learning about cases.</p>

  <h2> Nominative </h2>
  
  <p> The <b> nominative case </b> represents the subject of the sentence. The subject is usually the person or thing that is completing an action. (doing the verb). <br> </p>
  
  <h4> Examples </h4>
  
  <p> (a) <b> He </b> is <b>eating</b> an <b>apple </b>.</p> 
  
  <p lang="de"> (b) <b>Er </b> <b>isst</b> einen <b>Apfel </b>. </p> 
  
  <br>
  
  <p> (a) <b> She </b> is <b> flying </b> to <b> Germany </b> </p>
  
  <p lang="de" > (b) <b>Sie</b> <b>fliegt</b> nach <b>Deutschland</b>. </p>  
  
  <hr>
  
  <h2> Accusative </h2>
  
  <p> Accusative case is used when the noun in the sentence is the direct object. To put it simpler, accusative happens when the <b>direct object is the thing being affected directly by the verb.</b> </p>
  
  <h4> Examples </h4>
  
  <p> (a) The woman is <b>buying a skirt </b>. </p>
  
  <p lang="de"> (b) Die Frau <b>kauft einen Rock</b>.
  
  <br>
  
  <p> (a) The man <b>throws the ball</b>. </p> 
  
  <p lang="de"> (b) Der Mann <b>wirft einen Ball</b>. </p>
  
  <hr>
  
  <h2> Dative </h2>
  
  <p> Dative in German is the case that introduces an indirect object. If you are ever stuck in trying to determine which case the sentence is, to determine the dative case you would ask yourself the question <b> 'to whom?'</b> </p>
  
  <p> The dog throws the ball to me. <b> To whom does the dog throw the ball ? </b> - To me. </p>
  
  <h4> Examples </h4>
  
  <p> (a) The girl <b>gives the boy</b> a kiss. </p>
  <p lang="de"> (b) Das MÃ¤dchen <b>gibt dem Jungen </b> einen Kuss. </p>
  
  <br>
  
  <p> (a) The dog gives <b>the owner</b> the ball.</p>
  <p lang="de"> (b) Der Hund gibt <b>dem Besitzer</b> den Ball. </p> 
  
  <hr>
  
  <h2> Genitive </h2>
  
  <p> The genitive case personally is the case that is easier to comprehend that the others as the only use of the genitive case is to <b> show possesion of something. </b> </p>
  
  <h4> Examples </h4> 
  
  <p> (a) That is <b>my father's car.</b> </p>
  <p lang="de"> (b) Das ist <b>das Auto meines Vater.</b> </p>
  
  <br>
  
  <p> (a) The <b>man's job</b> is a teacher.</p>
  <p lang="de"> (b) <b>Der Beruf des Mannes</b> ist Lehrer. </p>
